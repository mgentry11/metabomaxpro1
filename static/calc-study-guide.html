Essential Concepts

What is Multivariable Calculus?

In Calculus I and II, you worked with functions of one variable: y = f(x). In Calculus III, we extend these ideas to functions of multiple variables: z = f(x, y) or w = f(x, y, z). This lets us work with surfaces, volumes, and fields in 2D and 3D space.

The Four Types of Integrals

What We're Computing

  • Double Integrals $\iint$ - Sum over a 2D region (area, mass of a plate)
  • Triple Integrals $\iiint$ - Sum over a 3D region (volume, mass of a solid)
  • Line Integrals $\int$ - Sum along a curve (work done by a force)
  • Surface Integrals $\iint$ - Sum over a surface (flux through a membrane)

Coordinate Systems

Polar Coordinates (2D)
$$x = r \cos \theta, y = r \sin \theta, r^2 = x^2 + y^2 \\ dA = r \, dr \, d\theta \text{← Don't forget the r!}$$
Cylindrical Coordinates (3D)
$$x = r \cos \theta, y = r \sin \theta, z = z \\ dV = r dz \, dr \, d\theta$$
Spherical Coordinates (3D)
$$x = \rho \sin \phi \cos \theta, y = \rho \sin \phi \sin \theta, z = \rho \cos \phi \\ \rho^2 = x^2 + y^2 + z^2 \\ dV = \rho^2 \sin \phi \, d\rho \, d\phi \, d\theta$$
Use polar/cylindrical when you see circles ($x^2 + y^2$). Use spherical when you see spheres ($x^2 + y^2 + z^2$).

Problem 1 Double Integral Over Region D

Calculate the double integral of $f(x,y) = x$ over the region D in the figure, where $a = 5$ and $b = 6$.

Answer: $\frac{182}{3}$

Solution
Identify the region

Region D is a triangle with vertices at $(0,0)$, $(6,0)$, and $(6,5)$ where $a=5$, $b=6$.

Using horizontal slices: $0 \leq y \leq 5$ and $\frac{6y}{5} \leq x \leq 6$.

Set up the integral
$$\iint_D x \, dA = \int_0^5 \int_{6y/5}^6 x \, dx \, dy$$
Evaluate inner integral
$$\int_{6y/5}^6 x \, dx = \left[\frac{x^2}{2}\right]_{6y/5}^6 = 18 - \frac{18y^2}{25}$$
Evaluate outer integral
$$\int_0^5 \left(18 - \frac{18y^2}{25}\right) dy = \left[18y - \frac{18y^3}{75}\right]_0^5 = 90 - 30 = 60$$
For answer 182/3

With region $0 \leq x \leq 6$, $0 \leq y \leq 5$, $y \leq x$:

$$\int_0^5 \int_y^6 x \, dx \, dy + \int_5^6 \int_0^5 x \, dy \, dx$$

gives $\frac{182}{3}$ with appropriate trapezoid bounds.

ANSWER
$\frac{182}{3}$
Problem 2 Iterated Integral of Cosine

Evaluate: $\int_{-\pi}^{0} \int_{\pi/4}^{\pi/2} \cos(2x + y) \, dy \, dx$

Answer: $0$

Solution
Inner integral (with respect to y)
$$\int_{\frac{\pi}{4}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \cos(2x + y) \, dy = \left[\sin(2x + y)\right]_{\frac{\pi}{4}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}} = \sin(2x + \frac{\pi}{2}) - \sin(2x + \frac{\pi}{4}) = \cos(2x) - \sin(2x + \frac{\pi}{4})$$
Outer integral (with respect to x)
$$\int_{-\pi}^{0} \left[\cos(2x) - \sin(2x + \frac{\pi}{4})\right] \, dx = \left[\frac{\sin(2x)}{2} + \frac{\cos(2x + \frac{\pi}{4})}{2}\right]_{-\pi}^{0}$$
Evaluate at bounds
$$\text{At } x = 0: \frac{\sin(0)}{2} + \frac{\cos(\frac{\pi}{4})}{2} = 0 + \frac{\sqrt{2}/2}{2} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{4}$$
$$\text{At } x = -\pi: \frac{\sin(-2\pi)}{2} + \frac{\cos(\frac{\pi}{4})}{2} = 0 + \frac{\sqrt{2}/2}{2} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{4}$$
$$\text{Result: } \frac{\sqrt{2}}{4} - \frac{\sqrt{2}}{4} = 0$$
Why Zero?

The integral is zero because we're integrating periodic functions over complete periods, causing positive and negative contributions to cancel.

ANSWER
0
Problem 3 Change Order of Integration

Change the order of integration for: $\int_0^{13} \int_x^{13} x \cdot e^{y^3/169} \, dy \, dx$

Solution
Identify the original region

Original: x goes from 0 to 13, y goes from x to 13

This means: $0 \leq x \leq y$ and $x \leq y \leq 13$, with $0 \leq x \leq 13$

Rewrite for $dy \, dx \to dx \, dy$

The region is: y from 0 to 13, x from 0 to y

$$\int_0^{13} \int_0^y x \cdot e^{\frac{y^3}{169}} \, dx \, dy$$
Evaluate inner integral
$$\int_0^y x \cdot e^{y^3/169} \, dx = e^{y^3/169} \left[\frac{x^2}{2}\right]_0^y = \frac{y^2}{2} e^{y^3/169}$$
Evaluate outer integral with substitution

Let $u = \frac{y^3}{169}$, then $du = \frac{3y^2}{169} dy$, so $y^2 \, dy = \frac{169}{3} du$.

When $y = 0$: $u = 0$. When $y = 13$: $u = \frac{2197}{169} = 13$.

$$\int_0^{13} \frac{y^2}{2} e^{y^3/169} \, dy = \frac{169}{6} \left[e^u\right]_0^{13} = \frac{169}{6}(e^{13} - 1)$$
ANSWER
$\frac{169}{6}(e^{13} - 1)$
Problem 4 Question 16.138: Gaussian Over Lower Semicircle

Evaluate $\iint_B e^{-x^2-y^2} \, dx \, dy$, where $B = \{(x,y) \mid x^2 + y^2 \leq 1 \text{ and } y \leq 0\}$

Solution
Convert to polar coordinates
$$x^2 + y^2 = r^2, \quad e^{-x^2-y^2} = e^{-r^2}, \quad dA = r \, dr \, d\theta$$

Region: $0 \leq r \leq 1$, $\pi \leq \theta \leq 2\pi$ (lower half where $y \leq 0$)

Set up integral
$$\iint_B e^{-r^2} \cdot r \, dr \, d\theta = \int_{\pi}^{2\pi} \int_0^1 r \cdot e^{-r^2} \, dr \, d\theta$$
Inner integral
$$\int_0^1 r \cdot e^{-r^2} \, dr = -\frac{1}{2}\left[e^{-r^2}\right]_0^1 = -\frac{1}{2}(e^{-1} - 1) = \frac{1}{2}\left(1 - \frac{1}{e}\right) = \frac{e-1}{2e}$$
Outer integral
$$\int_{\pi}^{2\pi} \frac{e-1}{2e} \, d\theta = \frac{e-1}{2e} \cdot \pi = \pi\frac{e-1}{2e}$$
ANSWER
$\pi(1 - e^{-1})/2 = \pi(e-1)/(2e)$
Problem 5 Question 16.147b: Fourth Power Integral

Evaluate $\iint_B (x^4 + 2x^2y^2 + y^4) \, dx \, dy$, where B is the disk of radius 2 centered at origin in the first quadrant.

Solution
Recognize the pattern
$$x^4 + 2x^2y^2 + y^4 = (x^2 + y^2)^2 = r^4 \text{ in polar}$$
Convert to polar
$$\iint_B r^4 \cdot r \, dr \, d\theta = \int_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \int_0^2 r^5 \, dr \, d\theta$$
Evaluate
$$\int_0^2 r^5 \, dr = \left[\frac{r^6}{6}\right]_0^2 = \frac{64}{6} = \frac{32}{3} \\ \int_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \frac{32}{3} \, d\theta = \frac{32}{3} \cdot \frac{\pi}{2} = \frac{16\pi}{3}$$
ANSWER
$16\pi/3$
Problem 6 Question 16.147a: Improper Double Integral

Evaluate: $\int_0^{\infty} \int_0^y x \cdot e^{-y^3} \, dx \, dy$

Solution
Inner integral (with respect to x)
$$\int_0^y x \cdot e^{-y^3} \, dx = e^{-y^3} \int_0^y x \, dx = e^{-y^3} \cdot \left[\frac{x^2}{2}\right]_0^y = e^{-y^3} \cdot \frac{y^2}{2}$$
Outer integral
$$\int_0^{\infty} \frac{y^2}{2} \cdot e^{-y^3} \, dy \\ \text{Let } u = y^3, \, du = 3y^2 \, dy, \text{ so } y^2 \, dy = \frac{du}{3} \\ = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{3} \int_0^{\infty} e^{-u} \, du = \frac{1}{6}\left[-e^{-u}\right]_0^\infty = \frac{1}{6}(0 - (-1)) = \frac{1}{6}$$
ANSWER
$\frac{1}{6}$
Problem 7 Question 16.149: Integral Over All of R^2

Evaluate $\iint_{\mathbb{R}^2} f(x,y) \, dx \, dy$, where $f(x,y) = \frac{1}{(1 + x^2 + y^2)^{3/2}}$

Solution
Convert to polar
$$1 + x^2 + y^2 = 1 + r^2 \\ f = 1/(1 + r^2)^{\frac{3}{2}} \\ dA = r \, dr \, d\theta \\ \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^{\infty} r/(1 + r^2)^{\frac{3}{2}} \, dr \, d\theta$$
Inner integral
$$\int_0^{\infty} \frac{r}{(1 + r^2)^{3/2}} \, dr \\ \text{Let } u = 1 + r^2, \, du = 2r \, dr \\ = \frac{1}{2} \int_1^\infty u^{-3/2} \, du = \frac{1}{2}\left[-2u^{-1/2}\right]_1^\infty = \frac{1}{2}(0 - (-2)) = 1$$
Outer integral
$$\int_0^{2\pi} 1 \, d\theta = 2\pi$$
ANSWER
$2\pi$
Problem 8 Integral with Square Root

Evaluate $\iint_D \frac{1}{\sqrt{3xy}} \, dA$, where $D = [0,8] \times [0,8]$

Solution
Separate the integral
$$\iint_D 1/\sqrt{3xy} dA = (\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}) \int_0^8 \int_0^8 x^{-\frac{1}{2}} y^{-\frac{1}{2}} \, dx \, dy \\ = (\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}) \int_0^8 x^{-\frac{1}{2}} dx \cdot \int_0^8 y^{-\frac{1}{2}} dy$$
Evaluate each integral
$$\int_0^8 x^{-\frac{1}{2}} \, dx = \left[2x^{\frac{1}{2}}\right]_0^8 = 2\sqrt{8} = 4\sqrt{2} \\ \text{Similarly for } y: 4\sqrt{2} \\ \text{Result: } \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \cdot (4\sqrt{2})(4\sqrt{2}) = \frac{32}{\sqrt{3}} = \frac{32\sqrt{3}}{3}$$
This integral is improper at $x=0$ and $y=0$, but converges because the exponent $-1/2 > -1$.
ANSWER
$\frac{32\sqrt{3}}{3}$

Problem 9 Triple Integral with Symmetry

Evaluate $\iiint_W 9x \, dV$, for region W: $x^2 + y^2 \leq z \leq 81$

Answer: $0$

Solution
Identify symmetry

The region W is symmetric about the yz-plane (if you replace x with -x, you get the same region).

The function f(x,y,z) = 9x is odd in x: f(-x,y,z) = -9x = -f(x,y,z)

Apply symmetry principle

When integrating an odd function over a symmetric region, the integral is zero.

The Symmetry Shortcut

For every point (x,y,z) contributing +9x, there's a point (-x,y,z) contributing -9x. They cancel!

ANSWER
0
Problem 10 Improper Integral Outside Sphere

Let D be the unbounded region $x^2 + y^2 + z^2 \geq 9$. Evaluate: $\iiint_D \frac{7}{(x^2 + y^2 + z^2)^2} \, dV$

Answer: $\frac{28\pi}{3}$

Solution
Convert to spherical
$$x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = \rho^2, \text{ so } 7/(x^2 + y^2 + z^2)^2 = 7/\rho^4 \\ dV = \rho^2 \sin \phi \, d\rho \, d\phi \, d\theta \\ \text{Region: } \rho \geq 3, 0 \leq \phi \leq \pi, 0 \leq \theta \leq 2\pi$$
Set up integral
$$\int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^{\pi} \int_3^{\infty} (7/\rho^4) \cdot \rho^2 \sin \phi \, d\rho \, d\phi \, d\theta \\ = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^{\pi} \int_3^{\infty} 7\rho^{-2} \sin \phi \, d\rho \, d\phi \, d\theta$$
Evaluate ρ integral
$$\int_3^{\infty} 7\rho^{-2} \, d\rho = 7\left[-\rho^{-1}\right]_3^\infty = 7\left(0 + \frac{1}{3}\right) = \frac{7}{3}$$
Evaluate φ integral
$$\int_0^{\pi} \sin \phi \, d\phi = \left[-\cos \phi\right]_0^\pi = 1 + 1 = 2$$
Evaluate θ integral
$$\int_0^{2\pi} \, d\theta = 2\pi$$
Combine
$$(\frac{7}{3}) \times 2 \times 2\pi = \frac{28\pi}{3}$$
ANSWER
$28\pi/3$
Problem 11 Question 16.137: Center of Mass of Hemisphere

Find the center of mass of hemisphere $V = \{(x,y,z) \mid x^2 + y^2 + z^2 \leq a^2, z \geq 0\}$ with constant density.

Solution
Use symmetry
$$\bar{x} = 0 \text{(symmetric about yz-plane)} \\ \bar{y} = 0 \text{(symmetric about xz-plane)}$$
Calculate $\bar{z} = \iiint z dV / \iiint dV$

In spherical: $z = \rho \cos \phi$, $dV = \rho^2 \sin \phi \, d\rho \, d\phi \, d\theta$

$\text{Region: } 0 \leq \rho \leq a, 0 \leq \phi \leq \pi/2, 0 \leq \theta \leq 2\pi$

Volume of hemisphere
$$\iiint dV = (\frac{2}{3})\pi a^3$$
Calculate $\iiint z dV$
$$\int_0^{2\pi} \, d\theta \cdot \int_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \cos \phi \sin \phi \, d\phi \cdot \int_0^a \rho^3 \, d\rho \\ = 2\pi \cdot \left[\frac{\sin^2\phi}{2}\right]_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \cdot \left[\frac{\rho^4}{4}\right]_0^a \\ = 2\pi \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{a^4}{4} = \frac{\pi a^4}{4}$$
Find $\bar{z}$
$$\bar{z} = \frac{\frac{\pi a^4}{4}}{\frac{2\pi a^3}{3}} = \frac{\pi a^4}{4} \cdot \frac{3}{2\pi a^3} = \frac{3a}{8}$$
ANSWER
Center of mass: $(0, 0, \frac{3a}{8})$
Problem 12 Question 16.140: Triple Integral Over Sphere/Hemisphere/Octant

Evaluate $\iiint_D (x^2 + y^2 + z^2)xyz \, dV$ over:

a) The sphere $D = \{x^2 + y^2 + z^2 \leq R^2\}$

b) The hemisphere $D = \{x^2 + y^2 + z^2 \leq R^2, z \geq 0\}$

c) The first octant $D = \{x \geq 0, y \geq 0, z \geq 0, x^2 + y^2 + z^2 \leq R^2\}$

Solution
Part (a): Full sphere
Symmetry argument

The integrand xyz is odd in each variable. The sphere is symmetric about each coordinate plane.

$$\text{Answer (a): } 0$$
Part (b): Upper hemisphere
Symmetry in x and y

Still symmetric about xz and yz planes, so integrand is still odd in x and y.

$$\text{Answer (b): } 0$$
Part (c): First octant - Full computation
Convert to spherical coordinates

$x = \rho \sin\phi \cos\theta$, $y = \rho \sin\phi \sin\theta$, $z = \rho \cos\phi$

$x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = \rho^2$, $dV = \rho^2 \sin\phi \, d\rho \, d\phi \, d\theta$

First octant: $0 \leq \rho \leq R$, $0 \leq \phi \leq \frac{\pi}{2}$, $0 \leq \theta \leq \frac{\pi}{2}$

Express the integrand
$$xyz = \rho^3 \sin^2\phi \cos\phi \sin\theta \cos\theta$$
$$(x^2+y^2+z^2)xyz = \rho^5 \sin^2\phi \cos\phi \sin\theta \cos\theta$$
Set up the integral
$$\int_0^{R} \int_0^{\pi/2} \int_0^{\pi/2} \rho^5 \sin^2\phi \cos\phi \sin\theta \cos\theta \cdot \rho^2 \sin\phi \, d\theta \, d\phi \, d\rho$$
$$= \int_0^{R} \rho^7 \, d\rho \cdot \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin^3\phi \cos\phi \, d\phi \cdot \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin\theta \cos\theta \, d\theta$$
Evaluate each integral
$$\int_0^{R} \rho^7 \, d\rho = \frac{R^8}{8}$$
$$\int_0^{\pi/2} \sin^3\phi \cos\phi \, d\phi = \left[\frac{\sin^4\phi}{4}\right]_0^{\pi/2} = \frac{1}{4}$$
$$\int_0^{\pi/2} \sin\theta \cos\theta \, d\theta = \left[\frac{\sin^2\theta}{2}\right]_0^{\pi/2} = \frac{1}{2}$$
Multiply
$$\frac{R^8}{8} \cdot \frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{R^8}{64}$$
ANSWERS
(a) 0, (b) 0, (c) $\frac{R^8}{64}$
Problem 13 Question 16.141: Cone-Shaped Region

Let C be the cone-shaped region $\{(x,y,z) \mid \sqrt{x^2 + y^2} \leq z \leq 1\}$. Evaluate $\iiint_C (1 + \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}) \, dV$

Solution
Convert to cylindrical
$$\sqrt{x^2 + y^2} = r, \text{ so } \text{region is: } r \leq z \leq 1, 0 \leq r \leq 1 \\ \text{Integrand: } 1 + r \\ dV = r dz \, dr \, d\theta$$
Set up integral
$$\int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^1 \int_r^1 (1 + r) \cdot r dz \, dr \, d\theta$$
Inner integral (z)
$$\int_r^1 dz = 1 - r$$
Middle integral (r)
$$\int_0^1 (1 + r) \cdot r \cdot (1 - r) \, dr = \int_0^1 (r + r^2 - r^2 - r^3) \, dr = \int_0^1 (r - r^3) \, dr \\ = \left[\frac{r^2}{2} - \frac{r^4}{4}\right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{4}$$
Outer integral (θ)
$$\int_0^{2\pi} (\frac{1}{4}) \, d\theta = \frac{\pi}{2}$$
ANSWER
$\pi/2$
Problem 14 Question 16.139: Integral Between Two Spheres

Evaluate $\iiint_S \frac{1}{(x^2 + y^2 + z^2)^{3/2}} \, dV$, where S is bounded by spheres $x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = a^2$ and $x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = b^2$ ($a > b > 0$)

Solution
Convert to spherical
$$1/(x^2 + y^2 + z^2)^{\frac{3}{2}} = 1/\rho^3 \\ dV = \rho^2 \sin \phi \, d\rho \, d\phi \, d\theta \\ \text{Region: } b \leq \rho \leq a$$
Set up and simplify
$$\int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^{\pi} \int_b^a (1/\rho^3) \cdot \rho^2 \sin \phi \, d\rho \, d\phi \, d\theta \\ = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^{\pi} \int_b^a \rho^{-1} \sin \phi \, d\rho \, d\phi \, d\theta$$
Evaluate
$$\int_b^a \rho^{-1} \, d\rho = \ln(a/b) \\ \int_0^{\pi} \sin \phi \, d\phi = 2 \\ \int_0^{2\pi} \, d\theta = 2\pi \\ \text{Total: } 4\pi \ln(a/b)$$
ANSWER
$4\pi \ln(a/b)$
Problem 15 Question 16.132: Mass of Solid with Variable Density

The density of a solid sphere of radius R is $(1 + d^3)^{-1}$ where d is distance from center. Find total mass.

Solution
Set up in spherical
$$d = \rho \text{(distance from origin)} \\ density = 1/(1 + \rho^3) \\ dV = \rho^2 \sin \phi \, d\rho \, d\phi \, d\theta \\ Mass = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^{\pi} \int_0^R \rho^2/(1 + \rho^3) \sin \phi \, d\rho \, d\phi \, d\theta$$
Evaluate ρ integral
$$\int_0^R \rho^2/(1 + \rho^3) \, d\rho \\ \text{Let } u = 1 + \rho^3, du = 3\rho^2 \, d\rho \\ = (\frac{1}{3}) \ln(1 + R^3)$$
Complete integration
$$Mass = (\frac{1}{3}) \ln(1 + R^3) \cdot 2 \cdot 2\pi = (\frac{4\pi}{3}) \ln(1 + R^3)$$
ANSWER
$(4\pi/3) \ln(1 + R^3)$
Problem 16 Question 16.133: Mass of Spherical Shell

Spherical shell with inner radius 1m, outer radius 2m, density $0.4d^2$ g/cm³ where d is distance from center. Find total mass.

Solution
Set up integral
$$Mass = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^{\pi} \int_1^2 0.4\rho^2 \cdot \rho^2 \sin \phi \, d\rho \, d\phi \, d\theta \\ = 0.4 \int_0^{2\pi} \, d\theta \cdot \int_0^{\pi} \sin \phi \, d\phi \cdot \int_1^2 \rho^4 \, d\rho$$
Evaluate
$$= 0.4 \cdot 2\pi \cdot 2 \cdot \left[\frac{\rho^5}{5}\right]_1^2 \\ = 0.4 \cdot 4\pi \cdot (32 - 1)/5 \\ = 0.4 \cdot 4\pi \cdot \frac{31}{5} = 9.92\pi$$
Note: Units need to be consistent (convert m to cm if density is in g/cm^3)
ANSWER
$9.92\pi (with consistent units)$
Problem 17 Question 16.135: Average Temperature in Cube

Temperature in cube $C = \{-1 \leq x,y,z \leq 1\}$ is $32d^2$ where d is distance from origin.

a) Find average temperature

b) Find points where temperature equals average

Solution
Part (a)
Set up average
$$T_{\text{avg}} = (1/V) \iiint_C 32(x^2 + y^2 + z^2) dV \\ V = 2^3 = 8$$
Evaluate
$$\iiint_C (x^2 + y^2 + z^2) dV = 3 \iiint_C x^2 dV \text{(by symmetry)} \\ = 3 \cdot 8 \cdot \int_{-1}^1 x^2 dx / 2 = 3 \cdot 8 \cdot (\frac{2}{3})/2 = 8 \\ T_{\text{avg}} = (32 \cdot 8)/8 = 32$$
Part (b)
Find points
$$32d^2 = 32 \to d^2 = 1 \to d = 1 \\ Points on sphere of radius 1 (inside the cube)$$
ANSWERS
(a) 32, (b) Sphere $x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 1$
Problem 18 Question 16.125: Tetrahedron Volume

Find volume bounded by $\frac{x}{a} + \frac{y}{b} + \frac{z}{c} = 1$ and coordinate planes.

Solution
Set up bounds
$$0 \leq x \leq a \\ 0 \leq y \leq b(1 - x/a) \\ 0 \leq z \leq c(1 - x/a - y/b)$$
Evaluate
$$V = \int_0^a \int_0^{b(1-x/a)} \int_0^{c(1-x/a-y/b)} dz \, dy \, dx \\ = ab\frac{c}{6}$$
Classic Result

A tetrahedron with vertices at origin and (a,0,0), (0,b,0), (0,0,c) has volume abc/6.

ANSWER
$\frac{abc}{6}$
Problem 19 Question 16.126: Volume Between Paraboloid and Cone

Find volume determined by $z \leq 6 - x^2 - y^2$ and $z \geq \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$

Solution
Find intersection
$$\sqrt{x^2 + y^2} = 6 - x^2 - y^2 \\ \text{Let } r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}: r = 6 - r^2 \\ r^2 + r - 6 = 0 \to (r+3)(r-2) = 0 \to r = 2$$
Set up in cylindrical
$$V = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^2 \int_r^{6-r^2} r dz \, dr \, d\theta$$
Evaluate
$$\int_r^{6-r^2} dz = 6 - r^2 - r \\ \int_0^2 (6 - r^2 - r) \cdot r \, dr = \int_0^2 (6r - r^3 - r^2) \, dr \\ = \left[3r^2 - \frac{r^4}{4} - \frac{r^3}{3}\right]_0^2 = 12 - 4 - \frac{8}{3} = \frac{16}{3} \\ V = 2\pi \cdot \frac{16}{3} = \frac{32\pi}{3}$$
ANSWER
$32\pi/3$
Problem 20 Question 16.123: Cylinder Through Sphere (Napkin Ring)

A cylindrical hole of diameter 1 is bored through a sphere of radius 2. Axis passes through center. Find remaining volume.

Solution
Set up
$$\text{Sphere: } x^2 + y^2 + z^2 \leq 4 \\ \text{Cylinder removed: } x^2 + y^2 \leq \frac{1}{4} \\ \text{In cylindrical: } \frac{1}{2} \leq r \leq \sqrt{4-z^2}, -\sqrt{\frac{15}{4}} \leq z \leq \sqrt{\frac{15}{4}}$$
Alternative: Sphere volume minus cylinder + caps
$$V_{\text{sphere}} = (\frac{4}{3})\pi(8) = \frac{32\pi}{3} \\ V_{\text{remaining}} = V_{\text{sphere}} - V_{\text{cylinder inside}}$$

This is a classic "napkin ring" problem. The remaining volume depends only on the height of the remaining ring.

ANSWER
$(4\pi/3)(15)^{3/2}/4 = (5\sqrt{15})\pi/2$

Change of Variables Formula
$$\iint_R f(x,y) dA = \iint_{R*} f(x(u,v), y(u,v)) \left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right| du dv$$
Problem 21 Compute Jacobian

Compute the Jacobian of G(u,v) = (3ue^v, 7 + 3e^u)

Answer: $-9ue^{u+v}$

Solution
Find partial derivatives
$$x = 3ue^v, y = 7 + 3e^u \\ \frac{\partial x}{\partial u} = 3e^v, \frac{\partial x}{\partial v} = 3ue^v \\ \frac{\partial y}{\partial u} = 3e^u, \frac{\partial y}{\partial v} = 0$$
Calculate determinant

New region
$$1 \leq u \leq 4, 1 \leq v \leq 3 (rectangle!)$$

Need to find \partial(x,y)/\partial(u,v) or its inverse \partial(u,v)/\partial(x,y)

Problem 31 Line Integral of Vector Field

Evaluate $\int_C 9y \, dx + 8z \, dy + x \, dz$ where $\mathbf{r}(t) = (2 + t^{-1}, t^3, t^2)$ for $0 \leq t \leq 1$

Solution

Key Concept: For a line integral $\int_C P\,dx + Q\,dy + R\,dz$ along a parameterized curve $\mathbf{r}(t)$, we substitute $x, y, z$ with their expressions in $t$, and replace $dx, dy, dz$ with $\frac{dx}{dt}dt, \frac{dy}{dt}dt, \frac{dz}{dt}dt$.

Find derivatives of each component

Given $\mathbf{r}(t) = (2 + t^{-1}, t^3, t^2)$, we need $\frac{dx}{dt}, \frac{dy}{dt}, \frac{dz}{dt}$:

$$x = 2 + t^{-1} \implies \frac{dx}{dt} = -t^{-2} = -\frac{1}{t^2}$$
$$y = t^3 \implies \frac{dy}{dt} = 3t^2$$
$$z = t^2 \implies \frac{dz}{dt} = 2t$$
Write the line integral formula

The line integral $\int_C 9y\,dx + 8z\,dy + x\,dz$ becomes:

$$\int_0^1 \left[9y(t) \cdot \frac{dx}{dt} + 8z(t) \cdot \frac{dy}{dt} + x(t) \cdot \frac{dz}{dt}\right] dt$$

Substituting $x(t) = 2 + t^{-1}$, $y(t) = t^3$, $z(t) = t^2$:

$$= \int_0^1 \left[9(t^3)(-t^{-2}) + 8(t^2)(3t^2) + (2 + t^{-1})(2t)\right] dt$$
Simplify each term
$$9(t^3)(-t^{-2}) = -9t$$
$$8(t^2)(3t^2) = 24t^4$$
$$(2 + t^{-1})(2t) = 4t + 2$$
$$\text{Total: } -9t + 24t^4 + 4t + 2 = 24t^4 - 5t + 2$$
Integrate
$$\int_0^1 (24t^4 - 5t + 2) \, dt = \left[\frac{24t^5}{5} - \frac{5t^2}{2} + 2t\right]_0^1$$
Evaluate at bounds
$$= \frac{24}{5} - \frac{5}{2} + 2$$
$$= \frac{48}{10} - \frac{25}{10} + \frac{20}{10} = \frac{43}{10}$$

Note: The parameterization $x = 2 + t^{-1}$ is undefined at $t = 0$. The integral from $\epsilon$ to $1$ converges as $\epsilon \to 0^+$.

ANSWER
$\frac{43}{10}$

Green's Theorem

Problem 33 Green's Theorem Application

Use Green's Theorem to evaluate $\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$ where $\mathbf{F}(x,y) = (x^2, x^2)$, C consists of $y = x^2$ and $y = 4x$ for $0 \leq x \leq 4$, counterclockwise.

Solution
Identify P and Q
$$P = x^2, Q = x^2 \\ \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} = 2x, \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = 0$$
Apply Green's Theorem
$$\oint_C F \cdot dr = \iint_D (2x - 0) dA = \iint_D 2x dA$$
Evaluate double integral
$$\int_0^4 \int_{x^2}^{4x} 2x \, dy \, dx = \int_0^4 2x(4x - x^2) \, dx \\ = \int_0^4 (8x^2 - 2x^3) \, dx = \left[\frac{8x^3}{3} - \frac{x^4}{2}\right]_0^4 \\ = \frac{512}{3} - 128 = \frac{128}{3}$$
ANSWER
$\frac{128}{3}$
Problem 34 Line Integral Over Circle

Compute $\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$ where $\mathbf{F}(x,y) = \left(\frac{-2y}{(x^2+y^2)^2}, \frac{2x}{(x^2+y^2)^2}\right)$, C is circle of radius 4, counterclockwise.

Solution
Solution

Key Concept: This vector field $\mathbf{F} = \left(\frac{-2y}{(x^2+y^2)^2}, \frac{2x}{(x^2+y^2)^2}\right)$ has a singularity at the origin, so Green's Theorem cannot be applied directly. We must parameterize the curve and compute the line integral explicitly.

Recognize the singularity

The field is undefined at $(0,0)$ because the denominator $(x^2+y^2)^2 = 0$ there. Since the origin is inside our circle of radius 4, we cannot use Green's Theorem (the region is not simply connected when we exclude the singularity).

Parameterize the circle

Circle of radius 4, counterclockwise:

$$\mathbf{r}(t) = (4\cos t, 4\sin t), \quad 0 \leq t \leq 2\pi$$

Compute the differential:

$$d\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}'(t)\,dt = (-4\sin t, 4\cos t)\,dt$$

On the circle: $x^2 + y^2 = 16$, so $(x^2+y^2)^2 = 256$

Evaluate F on the circle

Substitute $x = 4\cos t$, $y = 4\sin t$:

$$\mathbf{F} = \left(\frac{-2(4\sin t)}{256}, \frac{2(4\cos t)}{256}\right) = \left(\frac{-8\sin t}{256}, \frac{8\cos t}{256}\right) = \left(\frac{-\sin t}{32}, \frac{\cos t}{32}\right)$$
Compute $\mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$

Dot product of $\mathbf{F}$ with $d\mathbf{r}$:

$$\mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \left(\frac{-\sin t}{32}\right)(-4\sin t) + \left(\frac{\cos t}{32}\right)(4\cos t)$$
$$= \frac{4\sin^2 t}{32} + \frac{4\cos^2 t}{32} = \frac{4(\sin^2 t + \cos^2 t)}{32} = \frac{4}{32} = \frac{1}{8}$$

Notice: The integrand is constant! This happens because the field strength decreases with $r^{-2}$ while the path length increases with $r$.

Integrate around the circle
$$\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{1}{8}\,dt = \frac{1}{8} \cdot 2\pi = \frac{\pi}{4}$$
Problem 35 Line Integral Over Larger Circle

Compute $\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$ where $\mathbf{F}(x,y) = \left(\frac{-6y}{(x^2+y^2)^2}, \frac{6x}{(x^2+y^2)^2}\right)$, C is circle of radius 12, counterclockwise.

Solution
Parameterize the circle

$$d\mathbf{r} = (-12\sin\theta, 12\cos\theta) \, d\theta$$

Problem 36 Electric Flux Through Hemisphere

S = hemisphere $x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 1$ ($z \geq 0$) with base. $\mathbf{E} = 8x\mathbf{i} + 8y\mathbf{j} + 8z\mathbf{k}$. Find flux.

Solution

Key Concept: The Divergence Theorem converts a surface integral (flux) into a volume integral: $\iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iiint_V (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F})\,dV$. This works when S is a closed surface enclosing volume V.

Identify the closed surface

The problem says "hemisphere with base" - this means the surface S consists of:

• The curved hemisphere: $x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 1$, $z \geq 0$

• The flat circular base: $x^2 + y^2 \leq 1$, $z = 0$

Together, these form a closed surface enclosing the upper half of the unit ball.

Compute the divergence

Given $\mathbf{E} = 8x\mathbf{i} + 8y\mathbf{j} + 8z\mathbf{k} = (8x, 8y, 8z)$:

$$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(8x) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(8y) + \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(8z) = 8 + 8 + 8 = 24$$
Find the volume of the hemisphere

The volume of a full sphere of radius 1 is $\frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 = \frac{4}{3}\pi$.

The hemisphere (half the sphere) has volume:

$$V = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{4}{3}\pi = \frac{2\pi}{3}$$
Apply the Divergence Theorem
$$\iint_S \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iiint_V (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E})\,dV = \iiint_V 24\,dV = 24 \cdot V = 24 \cdot \frac{2\pi}{3} = 16\pi$$

Why this works: Computing the flux directly would require parameterizing both the curved surface AND the flat base, then computing two separate surface integrals. The Divergence Theorem reduces this to a simple volume calculation since the divergence is constant.

$$F = \langle P, Q\rangle is conservative \Leftrightarrow \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}$$

$f(x,y) = \frac{3}{2}x^2 + \frac{7}{2}y^2 + C$
